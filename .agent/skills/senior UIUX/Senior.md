# PART 1 — THE BIG PICTURE

# The Core Shift Happening in Design

AI did not kill design.

AI automated:
- repetitive execution
- average visual production
- generic UI generation
- surface-level ideation

The new advantage is no longer:
- making screens
- writing prompts
- generating layouts

The new advantage is:
- judgment
- taste
- systems thinking
- storytelling
- product reasoning
- decision-making
- refinement
- understanding human psychology

Execution is becoming cheaper.
Thinking is becoming more valuable.

---

# What Separates Senior Designers From Beginners

Beginners:
- design screens
- focus on aesthetics only
- optimize locally
- copy trends
- emphasize everything
- think in components
- design happy paths

Senior designers:
- design systems
- think in flows
- optimize journeys
- understand context
- design hierarchy intentionally
- understand second-order effects
- think about business + users together
- design edge cases and failures

Core principle:

> A beginner designs what they can see.
> A senior designer designs what they can predict.

# Important UX Laws

## 1. Aesthetic Usability Effect

Beautiful products feel easier to use.

Users perceive visually polished interfaces as more trustworthy.

---

## 2. Doherty Threshold

Fast feedback improves engagement.

Use:
- loading states
- skeleton screens
- progress indicators

---

## 3. Fitts’s Law

Large, nearby targets are easier to interact with.

Optimize:
- touch targets
- spacing
- thumb reach

---

## 4. Goal Gradient Effect

People accelerate when nearing completion.

Use:
- progress bars
- step indicators
- onboarding progress

---

## 5. Hick’s Law

Too many choices increase cognitive load.

Reduce:
- unnecessary decisions
- clutter
- complexity

---

## 6. Jacob’s Law

Users expect familiar patterns.

Do not reinvent standard interactions unnecessarily.

---

## 7. Law of Similarity

Visually similar elements are perceived as related.

Use consistent:
- styles
- components
- visual grouping

---

## 8. Miller’s Law

Humans process limited information at once.

Chunk complex tasks into smaller steps.

---

## 9. Zeigarnik Effect

People remember unfinished tasks.

Use progress tracking to encourage completion.

---

## 10. Von Restorff Effect

Things that stand out become memorable.

Use contrast strategically.

---

## 11. Law of Prägnanz

Humans prefer simple interpretations.

Simplify visuals.
Reduce mental effort.

---

## 12. Tesler’s Law

Some complexity cannot be removed.

It can only be managed.

# PART 3 — PORTFOLIO STRATEGY FOR 2026

# Core Portfolio Truth

Your portfolio is NOT:
- a gallery
- a Behance dump
- a decoration showcase

Your portfolio is:

> proof of thinking.

---

# Recruiter Reality

Recruiters spend:
- ~6 seconds initially

Your portfolio competes against:
- dozens of tabs
- limited attention
- hiring fatigue

---

# Portfolio Structure

## 1. Hook Section

Immediately show:
- strongest project
- clear problem
- measurable result
- interactive prototype

Avoid:
- generic introductions
- long bios
- vague statements

---

## 2. Case Study Deep Dives

Use:
- 2–3 strong projects

NOT:
- 15 mediocre projects

---

## Every Case Study Must Answer

1. What was the problem?
2. How did you solve it?
3. What happened after shipping?

---

## Show Process

Recruiters want:
- reasoning
- tradeoffs
- decision-making
- iteration
- problem solving

NOT just final screens.

---

## Show AI Usage Transparently

Include:
- research assistance
- brainstorming
- moodboards
- image generation
- copy refinement
- workflow acceleration

But explain:
- your thinking
- your refinements
- your decisions

---

## 3. Proof Section

Include:
- metrics
- outcomes
- testimonials
- adoption
- shipped products

---

## Strong Metrics Example

Good:
"Reduced checkout abandonment by 34%"

Weak:
"Improved user experience"

---

# Portfolio Technical Requirements

## Mobile Optimization

Many recruiters review portfolios on phones.

Poor mobile UX = instant rejection.

---

## Performance Matters

Heavy animations that:
- lag
- block reading
- reduce usability

hurt your credibility.

---

## Interactivity Matters

Embed:
- prototypes
- live flows
- interactions

Static screenshots feel outdated.

---

## Small Details Matter

Examples:
- reading time
- dark mode
- project counters
- smooth transitions
- polished loading

These signal:
- care
- polish
- systems thinking

---

# Recommended Case Study Structure

## 1. Context
Explain background.

## 2. Problem Statement
Define problems clearly.

## 3. Process
Show reasoning and exploration.

## 4. Solution
Show final flows and systems.

## 5. Conclusion
Explain outcomes and next steps.

# PART 5 — MINDSET SHIFTS

# Important Mental Models

## 1. Context Before Verdict

No design is universally correct.

Always ask:
- who is using this?
- in what situation?
- what is the trust level?
- what is the goal?

---

## 2. Hierarchy Across The Flow

Design energy should change across journeys.

Example:
- discovery pages = high energy
- decision pages = calmer
- checkout pages = minimal and focused

---

## 3. Emphasis Is A Budget

If everything is emphasized:
- nothing stands out.

---

## 4. Design Systems Matter

Think in:
- reusable patterns
- scalable architecture
- consistency
- maintainability

---

## 5. Speed Matters

Nobody waits:
- for portfolios
- for apps
- for interactions

Responsiveness is part of UX.
